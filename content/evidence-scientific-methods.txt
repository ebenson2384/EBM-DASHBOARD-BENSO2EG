# Scientific Evidence: Methods & Acquisition Strategy
# Document your systematic approach to finding research evidence

## Research Question
**Context:** Studying how increasing workplace communication affects job performance in general organizations, using a mediation model where communication (X) improves employee engagement (M), which in turn improves job performance (Y).

**Primary Research Question:** What is the empirical evidence linking workplace communication quality/frequency to employee job performance outcomes?

**Secondary Research Question:** What mechanisms or mediators explain the relationship between communication and performance (specifically, does employee engagement mediate this relationship)?

## Selection Criteria

### Inclusion Criteria
**Studies WILL be included if they meet these requirements:**

1. **Study Design:** Quantitative research with statistical analysis (correlational, experimental, quasi-experimental, meta-analysis)
2. **Effect Size Reporting:** Must report correlation coefficient (r), standardized regression coefficient (β), or other effect size measure (d, OR, etc.)
3. **Population:** Working adults in organizational settings (employees, not students in simulated tasks)
4. **Sample Size:** N ≥ 100 participants (ensures adequate statistical power; smaller meta-analyses acceptable if aggregating multiple studies)
5. **Communication Measure:** Must measure workplace/organizational communication (supervisor-employee, internal communication, information sharing, etc.)
6. **Performance Measure:** Must measure individual or organizational performance, productivity, or related outcomes (engagement accepted as mediator)
7. **Peer Review:** Published in peer-reviewed journal or well-regarded academic working paper
8. **Accessibility:** Full text accessible through free databases, institutional repositories, or author contact
9. **Language:** English (resource constraint)
10. **Relevance:** Direct bearing on communication-engagement-performance relationship

### Exclusion Criteria  
**Studies will be EXCLUDED if they have:**

1. **Purely qualitative:** No quantitative effect sizes reported (case studies without statistics, interview-only studies)
2. **Non-workplace context:** Student samples in lab experiments without workplace relevance; non-organizational settings
3. **Inaccessible:** Paywalled articles without free access and author non-responsive to requests
4. **Communication not focal:** Studies where communication is minor variable or not adequately measured
5. **Outcome mismatch:** Studies measuring only attitudes/perceptions without performance/engagement outcomes
6. **Very small samples:** N < 100 for primary studies (except meta-analyses)
7. **Highly specialized:** Military, emergency response, or other contexts that don't generalize to typical workplace
8. **Poor quality:** Obvious methodological flaws, non-peer-reviewed blog posts/opinion pieces, predatory journals
9. **Duplicate publication:** Same data published multiple times (keep most comprehensive version)
10. **Language barrier:** Non-English publications (would include if translation available but resource constrained)

## Quality Assessment Approach
**Framework:** Will use CAT Manager systematic critical appraisal framework in evidence-scientific-appraisal.txt to evaluate each selected study across five dimensions: Design, Sample, Measurement, Bias, and Applicability.

### Research Design Hierarchy
**Prioritizing higher-quality evidence:**

1. **Systematic Reviews/Meta-analyses** [Highest quality] - Aggregate multiple studies; provide weighted average effect sizes; assess publication bias
2. **Randomized Controlled Trials** [High quality] - Strongest causal inference; rare in workplace communication research due to practical/ethical constraints
3. **Longitudinal/Panel Studies** [Medium-high quality] - Temporal ordering helps establish causality; controls for baseline
4. **Quasi-experimental Studies** [Medium quality] - Natural experiments or pre-post comparisons without full randomization
5. **Cross-sectional Correlational Studies** [Medium-low quality] - Most common design; useful for effect size estimation but limited causal inference
6. **Case Studies with Quantitative Data** [Lower quality but contextually valuable] - Rich detail but limited generalizability

**For this review:** Expect primarily cross-sectional correlational studies and hopefully 1-2 meta-analyses given the established nature of communication-performance research. Will weight meta-analyses most heavily in synthesis.

### Quality Criteria Checklist
**Minimum standards for inclusion:**

- [X] Clear research question/hypothesis stated
- [X] Appropriate methodology for research question
- [X] Adequate sample size (N ≥ 100 for primary studies)
- [X] Valid measurement tools (established scales preferred)
- [X] Appropriate statistical analysis for claims made
- [X] Clear reporting of results (effect sizes, confidence intervals, p-values)
- [X] Discussion of limitations and alternative explanations
- [X] Peer-reviewed publication venue

**Additional quality indicators (preferred but not required):**
- Control variables included (age, tenure, industry, etc.)
- Multiple informants (reduce common method bias)
- Theoretical framework guiding hypotheses
- Replication of prior findings
- Practical significance discussed alongside statistical significance

## Challenges and Limitations
**Honest assessment of search process constraints**

### Search Limitations

**1. Database Access Constraints**
- **Issue:** No institutional library access to Business Source Premier, PsycINFO, JSTOR, or Web of Science
- **Impact:** Missed business/psychology-focused databases most likely to have relevant organizational behavior research
- **Mitigation:** Relied heavily on Google Scholar (broad coverage), ResearchGate (author-uploaded papers), and AI assistance for literature identification
- **Residual Gap:** Likely missed some high-quality studies behind paywalls in top-tier journals

**2. Full-Text Accessibility**
- **Issue:** Many relevant articles identified via abstracts but full text paywalled ($30-50 per article)
- **Impact:** Reduced from ~35 potentially relevant studies to ~12 accessible studies
- **Mitigation:** Contacted authors via ResearchGate for PDFs (50% response rate); prioritized open-access journals
- **Residual Gap:** May have excluded excellent studies simply due to access barriers

**3. Search Term Limitations**
- **Issue:** "Communication" is broad term; may have missed studies using different terminology (e.g., "information sharing," "transparency," "feedback")
- **Impact:** Potential relevant studies not captured in searches
- **Mitigation:** Used multiple search strings with varied terminology; AI helped identify alternative terms
- **Residual Gap:** Likely missed some studies with non-standard communication terminology

**4. Recency Bias**
- **Issue:** Google Scholar and open access favor recent publications; older seminal works may be overlooked
- **Impact:** May underweight foundational studies from 1990s-2000s
- **Mitigation:** AI assistant specifically asked for "seminal" works; included highly-cited older papers when identified
- **Residual Gap:** Historical context for field development may be incomplete

### Resource Constraints

**1. Time Constraints**
- **Allocated Time:** ~6-8 hours total for literature search and review (class assignment timeframe)
- **Ideal Time:** Systematic review would require 40+ hours for comprehensive search
- **Impact:** Prioritized finding 2-5 high-quality studies over exhaustive search
- **Trade-off Accepted:** Focused depth over breadth - better to thoroughly analyze few good studies than superficially review many

**2. Expertise Limitations**
- **Issue:** Student conducting review, not professional systematic reviewer
- **Impact:** May have missed nuanced search strategies or quality assessment criteria experts would use
- **Mitigation:** Used structured frameworks (CAT Manager), consulted AI for methodology guidance
- **Residual Gap:** Professional librarian or systematic review expert would likely improve search quality

**3. Single Reviewer**
- **Issue:** Best practice for systematic reviews is 2+ independent reviewers for screening/selection
- **Impact:** Potential bias in inclusion/exclusion decisions; may miss relevant studies or include marginal ones
- **Mitigation:** Used explicit criteria checklist; documented decisions for transparency
- **Residual Gap:** Consensus process would reduce subjective judgment errors

### Topic-Specific Challenges

**1. Construct Definition Variability**
- **Issue:** "Communication" measured many ways - frequency, quality, openness, satisfaction, effectiveness, etc.
- **Impact:** Hard to directly compare effect sizes across studies with different communication constructs
- **Mitigation:** Documented how each study operationalized communication; grouped similar measures
- **Implication:** Meta-analysis would need to code communication subtypes

**2. Performance Measurement Heterogeneity**
- **Issue:** Performance measured as supervisor ratings, objective metrics, self-report, engagement, OCB, etc.
- **Impact:** Effect sizes not directly comparable across different performance operationalizations
- **Mitigation:** Differentiated task performance vs. contextual performance vs. engagement in evidence synthesis
- **Implication:** Need to qualify findings by outcome type

**3. Common Method Bias Prevalence**
- **Issue:** Many studies rely on single-source self-report surveys (employee reports both communication and own performance)
- **Impact:** Effect sizes likely inflated due to common method variance
- **Mitigation:** Prioritized studies with multiple informants or objective performance data where possible
- **Residual Gap:** May still overestimate true effect sizes

**4. Cross-sectional Design Dominance**
- **Issue:** Most accessible studies are cross-sectional correlational designs (communication and performance measured simultaneously)
- **Impact:** Cannot establish causal direction - does communication improve performance, or do high performers communicate more?
- **Mitigation:** Relied on longitudinal studies and experimental research where available; used theory to support causal interpretation
- **Residual Gap:** Causal claims remain tentative for most evidence

**5. Publication Bias**
- **Issue:** Studies finding positive communication-performance relationships more likely published than null findings
- **Impact:** Average effect size in literature likely overestimates true population effect
- **Mitigation:** Sought meta-analyses that test for publication bias; acknowledged limitation in interpretation
- **Residual Gap:** Cannot fully correct for "file drawer problem"

---

**TRANSPARENCY NOTE:** These limitations do not invalidate the evidence but require cautious interpretation. Effect sizes should be viewed as upper-bound estimates. Causal claims are theoretical rather than empirical. Generalizability may be limited to contexts similar to studied samples. These constraints are typical of student-conducted evidence reviews and acceptable given resource/time limitations, but a funded systematic review with full database access would strengthen conclusions.
