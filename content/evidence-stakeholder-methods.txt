# Stakeholder Evidence: Methods & Engagement Strategy
# Document your approach to gathering stakeholder perspectives and feedback

## Stakeholder Evidence Goals
**Primary Goal:** Identify which stakeholders are most affected by communication problems and have greatest interest/power to influence solution implementation; understand stakeholder perspectives on communication gaps, engagement impacts, and performance consequences; assess stakeholder receptivity to communication improvement initiatives

**Secondary Goal:** Gather stakeholder input on solution feasibility, resource requirements, implementation barriers, and success criteria; identify potential champions and resisters; understand competing priorities and organizational political dynamics that affect initiative success

## Target Stakeholder Groups

### Internal Stakeholders
#### Management/Leadership (Primary Stakeholder Group)
- **Target Participants:** Federal agency senior executives (SES level), HR Directors, Chief Human Capital Officers, Office Directors managing 50+ employees, supervisors who directly manage frontline employees
- **Engagement Method:** 
  * FEVS open-ended comments analysis (existing data - already captured in 2024 survey)
  * Federal News Network/Government Executive interviews with agency leaders (secondary source analysis)
  * OPM Chief Human Capital Officer Council meeting notes (publicly available)
- **Key Questions Focus:** 
  * Do leaders recognize communication as priority problem or view it as less important than other HR issues?
  * What barriers do leaders cite for supervisor communication improvement (time, training, accountability, culture)?
  * How do leaders balance communication investments vs. competing priorities (recruitment, retention, performance management)?
  * What success metrics do leaders want for communication initiatives?
- **Expected Response Rate:** N/A - Using existing public data sources rather than new primary data collection (graduate student constraint: no IRB approval, limited access to federal executives)

#### Employees/Staff (Primary Stakeholder Group)
- **Target Participants:** Federal employees across all grade levels (GS-5 through GS-15), supervisory and non-supervisory, diverse agencies and occupations
- **Engagement Method:** 
  * FEVS 2024 data (n=624,812 responses - comprehensive employee voice already captured)
  * FEVS open-ended comment analysis (qualitative insights on communication problems)
  * Federal employee union statements/newsletters on workplace communication
  * Federal workplace Reddit/GovLoop forums (anonymous employee discussions)
- **Key Questions Focus:**
  * What specific communication failures do employees experience (frequency, clarity, feedback, transparency)?
  * How do employees link communication quality to engagement and performance?
  * What communication improvements would employees most value?
  * What concerns do employees have about communication training initiatives (skepticism, past failed initiatives, cynicism)?
- **Expected Response Rate:** 40% (actual FEVS 2024 response rate: 624,812 of 1.5M employees surveyed)

#### HR Professionals (Secondary Stakeholder Group)
- **Target Participants:** Federal agency HR specialists, organizational development consultants, training managers
- **Engagement Method:**
  * SHRM Government Affairs statements on federal workforce issues (secondary source)
  * OPM training program descriptions and participation data (public data)
  * Federal HR practitioner conference presentations (FedTalent Exchange, CHCO Council)
- **Key Questions Focus:**
  * What HR capacity exists for supervisor communication training?
  * What past communication initiatives have succeeded/failed and why?
  * What implementation challenges do HR professionals anticipate?
- **Expected Response Rate:** N/A - Secondary source analysis

### External Stakeholders
#### Federal Service Recipients/Taxpayers (Tertiary Stakeholder Group)
- **Target Participants:** Citizens who interact with federal agencies (beneficiaries, regulated entities, general public)
- **Engagement Method:**
  * American Customer Satisfaction Index (ACSI) government data - links employee engagement to customer satisfaction
  * Agency-specific customer satisfaction surveys (publicly available)
  * Citizen feedback on federal services (USAGov feedback, agency comment periods)
- **Key Questions Focus:**
  * Do customers perceive service quality problems related to employee engagement?
  * How do customer satisfaction trends align with FEVS engagement trends?
- **Expected Response Rate:** N/A - Existing public data

#### Congress/Oversight Bodies (Secondary Stakeholder Group - High Power)
- **Target Participants:** Congressional committees overseeing federal workforce (House Oversight, Senate Homeland Security), Government Accountability Office (GAO), Office of Management & Budget (OMB)
- **Engagement Method:**
  * GAO reports on federal workforce management (publicly available)
  * Congressional hearing testimony on FEVS results
  * OMB management agenda documents
- **Key Questions Focus:**
  * What priority does Congressional oversight place on employee engagement?
  * What accountability mechanisms exist for FEVS improvement?
  * What budget/resource support available for workforce initiatives?
- **Expected Response Rate:** N/A - Public document analysis

## Data Collection Methods

### Primary Method: Secondary Analysis of Existing Stakeholder Data

**Rationale for Secondary Data Approach:**
- Graduate student constraints: No IRB approval for primary data collection; limited access to federal executives for interviews; no resources for large-scale stakeholder surveys
- FEVS 2024 provides comprehensive employee stakeholder voice (n=624,812) including open-ended comments with qualitative insights
- Public documents (GAO reports, Congressional testimony, agency strategic plans) provide leadership/oversight perspectives
- Ethical advantage: Leverages existing data without imposing additional burden on busy federal stakeholders

**Data Sources:**

1. **FEVS 2024 Open-Ended Comments** (Employee Voice)
   - Source: OPM public data file with redacted employee comments on "What should agency improve?"
   - Sample: ~200,000 text comments from 2024 survey respondents
   - Analysis: Text mining for communication-related themes (supervisor, feedback, transparency, listening)
   - Coding: Frequency of communication mentions; sentiment analysis (positive/negative); specific communication failure descriptions

2. **Federal News Network/Government Executive Leadership Interviews** (Management Perspective)
   - Source: Published interviews with federal HR leaders, agency directors, CHCO Council members
   - Sample: 15-20 leadership interviews from 2023-2024 on FEVS results and engagement initiatives
   - Analysis: Thematic analysis of leader perspectives on communication priorities, barriers, solutions
   - Focus: Identify implementation challenges leaders cite; resource allocation priorities; past initiative outcomes

3. **GAO Reports on Federal Workforce Management** (Oversight Perspective)
   - Source: Government Accountability Office public reports (2020-2024)
   - Sample: 5-8 reports on federal HR management, employee engagement, supervision quality
   - Analysis: Document review for stakeholder (Congressional) priorities, accountability recommendations, identified management weaknesses
   - Focus: Extract oversight body perspective on communication/engagement importance relative to other federal workforce issues

4. **ACSI Federal Government Customer Satisfaction Data** (External Stakeholder Perspective)
   - Source: American Customer Satisfaction Index quarterly government sector reports
   - Sample: Federal agency customer satisfaction scores 2020-2024
   - Analysis: Correlate FEVS engagement trends with customer satisfaction trends (test whether internal engagement affects external service quality)
   - Focus: Validate that employee engagement matters to external stakeholders (taxpayers/citizens)

### Supplementary Method: Stakeholder Power/Interest Mapping (Analytical Exercise)

**Power/Interest Matrix Development:**
- Plot stakeholders on 2x2 matrix: Power (ability to influence communication initiative) vs. Interest (stake in communication problem/solution)
- Data sources: Organizational charts (formal power), FEVS results (employee interest level), media coverage (public interest), budget authority (resource control power)
- Purpose: Identify key stakeholders requiring active engagement vs. monitoring vs. information-only strategies

**Stakeholder Categorization:**
- **High Power/High Interest:** Senior agency leaders, CHCOs, supervisors (directly affected and control implementation) = MANAGE CLOSELY
- **High Power/Low Interest:** OMB, Congressional appropriators (control resources but communication not top priority) = KEEP SATISFIED
- **Low Power/High Interest:** Frontline employees (highly affected but limited influence) = KEEP INFORMED
- **Low Power/Low Interest:** General public, external researchers = MONITOR

## Sampling Strategy

### Stakeholder Identification

**Stakeholder Universe:**
- Internal Primary: 1.5M federal employees (FEVS sampling frame)
- Internal Secondary: ~50,000 federal supervisors/managers
- Internal Tertiary: ~1,000 senior executives (SES)
- Internal Support: ~5,000 HR professionals
- External Primary: 330M U.S. citizens (federal service recipients)
- External Secondary: 535 Congressional members + oversight staff
- External Tertiary: Academic researchers, good government groups (Partnership for Public Service, etc.)

**Sampling Approach:**
- **Employees:** Use FEVS probability sample (n=624,812 from 1.5M population = 40% response rate; OPM stratified random sample ensures representation by agency, grade, demographics)
- **Leaders:** Purposive sampling of public statements (target agencies with best/worst FEVS scores; leaders who have publicly discussed engagement initiatives)
- **Oversight:** Census of relevant GAO reports (all federal workforce management reports 2020-2024)
- **Customers:** Use ACSI federal agency scores (covers major agencies serving public)

**Representation Goals:**
- Agency diversity: Cover civilian agencies (not just DOD), large (10K+) and small (<1K) agencies, mission variety (regulatory, service delivery, research)
- Demographic diversity: FEVS data includes age, race, gender, tenure, supervisory status - ensure all groups represented
- Perspective diversity: Employee vs. management vs. oversight vs. customer viewpoints
- Geographic diversity: Federal workforce across all regions (though FEVS doesn't report geographic breakdowns publicly)

### Recruitment Strategy

**N/A for Secondary Data Analysis** - No active recruitment needed since using existing public data sources

**Ethical Justification:** Secondary analysis of de-identified public data does not constitute human subjects research under federal regulations; no IRB approval required; no informed consent needed (data already publicly available); respects stakeholder time (no additional burden imposed)

## Ethical Considerations

### Informed Consent
**N/A - Secondary Data Analysis Only**
- All data sources are publicly available (FEVS published by OPM, GAO reports public, news interviews published with consent)
- No new primary data collection = no informed consent required
- FEVS respondents provided consent when completing OPM survey (separate process outside this project)
- Public figure interviews (agency leaders, Congress) already published with source consent

### Organizational Permissions
**Not Required for Graduate Academic Project Using Public Data**
- **Supervisor Approval:** N/A - Not collecting data within employing organization
- **HR Consultation:** N/A - Not surveying current employees
- **IRB Review:** Not required (confirmed: secondary analysis of publicly available de-identified data is NOT human subjects research under 45 CFR 46)
- **Data Access:** All FEVS data, GAO reports, ACSI scores publicly accessible without special permissions

### Privacy Protections
- **FEVS Data:** OPM publishes aggregate data only (agency-level, not individual responses); open-ended comments are redacted to remove identifying information
- **Published Interviews:** Sources already public; no additional privacy concerns
- **Analysis:** Will not attempt to re-identify individuals from aggregate data; will not combine data sources in ways that could identify specific respondents

### Research Ethics Principles Adherence
- **Beneficence:** Research aims to improve federal workplace communication/engagement - benefits stakeholders
- **Non-maleficence:** No risk to stakeholders (no new data collection, no sensitive information, no identifiable data)
- **Justice:** Using representative sample (FEVS) ensures all stakeholder groups have voice, not just easily accessible or powerful groups
- **Respect for Persons:** Secondary analysis respects stakeholder autonomy (doesn't impose research burden); original FEVS consent process respected

### Analysis Preparation

**Qualitative Coding Strategy (FEVS Open Comments):**
1. **Initial Coding:** Read 500 random comments to identify themes
2. **Codebook Development:** Create coding categories (communication problems, engagement impacts, solution suggestions, barriers)
3. **Deductive Codes:** Theory-driven (supervisor listening, feedback quality, transparency, trust)
4. **Inductive Codes:** Data-driven (emerging themes like remote work communication, generational differences, technology issues)
5. **Reliability:** Two coders independently code 100 comments subset; calculate inter-rater reliability (Cohen's kappa); aim for κ > 0.70
6. **Full Coding:** Apply codebook to full comment set (or random sample of 2,000-5,000 if full set too large)
7. **Frequency Analysis:** Count mentions of each code; identify most common stakeholder concerns

**Quantitative Analysis (FEVS Survey Data):**
- Stakeholder segmentation: Compare communication perceptions across demographic groups (supervisory status, tenure, age, agency)
- Priority analysis: Identify which stakeholder groups rate communication as most/least problematic
- Trend analysis: Track stakeholder sentiment on communication over 5-year period
- Correlation analysis: Test whether stakeholder interest (engagement) correlates with problem severity

**Stakeholder Mapping Analysis:**
- Power assessment: Evaluate each stakeholder group's ability to influence communication initiative (budget authority, implementation control, veto power)
- Interest assessment: Evaluate each stakeholder group's stake in problem/solution (direct impact, concern level, participation willingness)
- Strategy assignment: Assign engagement strategy based on Power/Interest quadrant
- Validation: Cross-check mapping against published organizational charts, budget documents, strategic plans

**Integration Plan:**
- **Triangulation:** Compare employee perspective (FEVS) vs. leadership perspective (interviews) vs. oversight perspective (GAO) - identify convergence and divergence
- **Synthesis:** Create unified stakeholder landscape showing who cares most about communication problem, who has power to implement solutions, who might resist, who needs convincing
- **Gap Analysis:** Identify stakeholder perspectives NOT captured in available data (e.g., private sector comparison, international perspectives)

---

**Methodological Note:** This stakeholder evidence strategy prioritizes feasibility (graduate student constraints, public data availability) over ideal primary data collection. Limitation: Stakeholder voice captured indirectly through existing sources rather than direct engagement. Strength: Leverages massive existing stakeholder input (n=624K employees via FEVS) that would be impossible to replicate through primary research.
